Three Wise Brothers

Once upon a time, there lived a poor man with three sons. He often said to his sons, "My sons! We have neither flocks nor gold, nothing at all. Therefore, you should accumulate a different kind of treasure‚Äîto try to understand and know more. Nothing should escape your notice. Instead of large flocks, you'll have keen eyes, and instead of gold, a sharp mind. Once you have accumulated such wealth, you will never lack anything and will not feel inferior to others."

As time passed, the old man passed away. The brothers gathered together, considered the situation, and then said, "There's nothing for us to do here. Let us travel and see the world. If necessary, we'll work as shepherds or farm labourers. No matter where we are, we will not go hungry."

They travelled through desolate valleys and climbed high mountains, and they kept walking like this for forty days.

Their food supply dwindled, and they grew tired; their feet were blistered, but the road appeared endless. Whenever they stopped to rest, they soon pressed onward.

Finally, they saw trees, towers, and houses ahead of them, indicating that they were approaching a large city.

The three brothers were delighted and began walking faster.

When they were very near the city, the eldest brother suddenly stopped, looked at the ground, and said‚Äî

"A very large camel passed by here just a short while ago."

When they went a little further, the middle brother stopped and looking at both sides of the road said‚Äî

"The camel was one-eyed."

When they went a little further, the youngest brother said‚Äî

"There was a woman and a child riding on the camel."

"Exactly!" said the two older brothers, and the three of them continued on their way. A short while later, a horseman passed them. The eldest brother looked at him and asked, "Horseman, are you looking for something you lost?" The horseman stopped his horse and replied.

"Yes."

"Your camel is lost, isn't it?" asked the eldest brother.

"Yes."

"A big one?" asked the eldest.

"Yes."

"He is one-eyed, isn't he?" the middle brother asked.

"Yes."

"A woman and a child were riding it too?" the youngest asked.

"Yes," confirmed the horseman, giving them a look filled with suspicion. "If you know so much, you must have stolen my camel! Tell me quickly, what did you do with it?"

"We haven't even seen the face of your camel," the brothers protested.

"So, how did you learn all these details about him?"

"Because we know how to use our eyes and brains," the brothers explained. "Drive your horse quickly in that direction; you'll surely find your camel."

"No," the camel owner responded, "I will not go in that direction. My camel is with you, and you must return it to me."

"We haven't even seen your camel," the brothers insisted.

But the horseman refused to listen. He drew his sword and, swinging it forcefully, directed the three brothers to walk ahead of him. Thus, he led them; he led them directly towards the king's palace. After handing them over to the guards, he approached the king himself.

"I was leading my flock into the mountains," he explained, "and my wife and son were following me on a large one-eyed camel. Somehow their camel got left behind, and they lost their way. I went to look for them and found three men walking on the road. I am certain they have stolen my camel and killed my wife and son."

"Why do you think so?" the king inquired after the man had finished speaking.

"Because I hadn't told them anything about it, they told me that the camel was very big and one-eyed and that a woman was riding on it with a child."

The king thought for a while before saying‚Äî

"If, as you claim, they described your camel in detail without you telling them, they must have stolen it. Go, bring those thieves here."

The camel's owner went out and hurried inside, taking the three brothers with him.

"Thieves, tell me immediately!" The king shouted in a threatening tone. "Answer immediately! Where have you gone with this man's camel?"

"We are not thieves, and we have never seen his camel," the brothers explained calmly.

Then the king said,

"You provided an entirely accurate account of the camel in detail, without the owner telling you anything. Now, how dare you claim you didn't steal it!"

"Your Majesty, there's nothing surprising about this," the brothers responded. "We've been taught since we were children not to miss anything. We have spent a lot of time observing things with sharp eyes and thinking with our minds. That's why we knew what the camel looked like without looking at it."

The king chuckled.

"Is it possible to know so much about something without ever seeing it?" he asked.
